Research is how we make sure that the products, services, and policies we create truly work for the people of New Jersey. It grounds our decisions in the lived experiences of real users — not assumptions or internal perspectives.

When we listen to people, we gain insight into what matters most to them, what challenges they face, and how government services can better meet their needs. This work helps us design better systems and builds trust by showing that residents' voices guide the process.

---

## Guiding principles

All research must be rooted in trauma-informed, ethical, and inclusive practices.

<CardGrid>
  <Card title="Safety" icon="shield">
    Participants should feel emotionally, physically, and psychologically safe throughout the process. We ask what they need to feel comfortable and take steps to prevent harm or re-traumatization.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Trust and transparency" icon="information">
    We explain our goals and methods in plain language, answer questions openly, and keep our commitments. Trust is built when expectations are clear and we follow through.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Choice" icon="approve-check-circle">
    Participants have control over how they take part in research. We offer options, respect their decisions, and recognize that people who have experienced trauma may value control even more.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Collaboration" icon="puzzle">
    We aim to share power in the process, working alongside participants and partners rather than treating them as subjects.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Empowerment and voice" icon="rocket">
    We validate participants' experiences, invite them to shape outcomes, and support them in making decisions and taking action.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Equity and inclusion" icon="star">
    We recognize cultural, historical, and gender disparities, and actively work to reduce bias in our teams, methods, and recruitment. Diverse voices strengthen our work.
  </Card>
</CardGrid>

## When and what kind of research to do

Before starting any research, ask yourself:

> **What do we need to learn right now to make the best decision?**

The answer guides both the phase of research you're in and the method you should use.

### Research phases

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="Discovery">
    ### Understanding the landscape

    At the start of a project, we don't yet know what we don't know. Discovery research helps us learn about the problem space, the users, and the context.

    **Best methods:** Interviews, observation, surveys, participatory research, journey mapping

    *Example: Exploring how Unemployment Insurance could be made more intuitive and equitable.*
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Problem Validation">
    ### Confirming assumptions

    Sometimes stakeholders think they know what the problem is. Validation ensures that users actually experience it the same way.

    **Best methods:** Interviews, surveys, observation

    *Example: A team believes document uploading is the biggest challenge. Do users agree?*
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Concept Testing">
    ### Checking direction

    After brainstorming, we test whether our concepts match user needs and expectations.

    **Best methods:** Moderated usability testing, observation, participatory research

    *Example: Testing whether a digital intake form would be welcomed by users.*
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Solution Testing">
    ### Making sure it works

    With prototypes in hand, we confirm whether the solution is easy to use and functions as expected.

    **Best methods:** Moderated and unmoderated usability testing

    *Example: Watching users navigate a Figma prototype to see if navigation is intuitive.*
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Scaling & Evaluation">
    ### Learning after launch

    Even after launch, analytics may reveal unexpected behavior or problems. Research helps uncover why.

    **Best methods:** Surveys, interviews, analytics review

    *Example: Increased calls about claim status after launching a new portal.*
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Research methods

<CardGrid>
  <Card title="Interviews" icon="open-book">

  </Card>
  <Card title="Observation & shadowing" icon="magnifier">

## Planning your research

A strong plan answers three things before you start:
1. **What do we need to learn?** — goals
2. **Who do we need to hear from?** — recruitment
3. **What resources do we need?** — budget and logistics

<Steps>

1. ### Define your goals

   Start with a clear question: *What do we need to learn in order to make a decision?*

   - Be specific. Instead of "understand users," write: "Learn what challenges residents face when uploading documents for Unemployment Insurance."
   - Connect goals to the phase of research you're in.
   - Share your goals with peers to make sure they're relevant and realistic.

2. ### Plan recruitment

   Aim for participants who represent the diversity of New Jersey across geography, age, gender, cultural background, language, and abilities.

   <Aside type="caution">
   We do not conduct research with participants who are currently in crisis or have been in crisis within the past 12 months.
   </Aside>

   Recruitment happens through **UserInterviews.com** — our primary platform for participant management.

3. ### Write good questions

   Questions should invite open, honest responses. Keep them short, clear, and neutral.

   | Instead of | Try |
   |---|---|
   | What do you like and dislike about the Anchor program? | Ask them separately: *What do you like?* then *What don't you like?* |
   | Do you prefer the option with the blue button? | Which option do you prefer and why? |
   | Do you like these colors? | Why do these colors work (or not work) for you? |

4. ### Budget for your research

   Every plan must include a budget. Think through all costs:

   - Participant compensation *(always required)*
   - Number of sessions *(always required)*
   - Travel and lodging if in-person *(requires pre-approval)*
   - Supplies and equipment if needed

   We use **UserInterviews for all participant compensation** — researchers no longer handle payments directly.

   **Compensation rates** (based on scheduled time):

   | Session length | Rate |
   |---|---|
   | 30 minutes | $45 |
   | 45 minutes | $60 |
   | 60 minutes | $75 |
   | Intercept interviews | $25 gift card (flat rate) |

</Steps>

## Review and approval

Every research project must be reviewed before it begins. This protects participants, ensures quality, and confirms resources are being used wisely.

<Steps>

1. ### Peer review

   Share your plan with an experienced researcher. Options include:
   - **Design Crit** on Wednesdays
   - The `#design-feedback` or `#design-research` Slack channels
   - A 1:1 review with a peer researcher

   Ask for feedback on ethical risks, recruitment diversity, related projects, and question quality.

   **Deliverable:** An email from the peer reviewer confirming all comments have been addressed and the plan is approved.

2. ### Director review

   Your plan goes to a Design Director for approval. They'll assess:
   - **Method** — Is this the right approach for what we need to learn?
   - **Value** — Does this research add meaningful insight?
   - **ROI** — Is this worth the time and budget requested?

   **Deliverable:** An email from the Design Director confirming approval of the plan and/or budget request.

3. ### Budget and compensation approval

   <Tabs>
     <TabItem label="Remote research">
       If total participant compensation exceeds **$500**, email the Chief of Staff and Director of Finance for approval. CC both design directors on the request.
     </TabItem>
     <TabItem label="In-person research">
       Email your full budget along with peer and director approvals to the Chief of Staff and Director of Finance. CC both design directors.

       If approved, submit your travel pre-authorization form. This budget should cover flights, lodging, [per diem](https://www.gsa.gov/travel/plan-book/per-diem-rates/per-diem-rates-results?action=perdiems_report&fiscal_year=2025&state=NJ&city=&zip=), and any necessary supplies.
     </TabItem>
   </Tabs>

</Steps>

<Aside type="tip" title="When time is short">
A director review can stand in for both peer review and Design Director approval. Allow at least **one business day**. Do not reschedule participant interviews if avoidable.
</Aside>

## Recruiting participants

<Aside type="note">
**Default rule: always start with UserInterviews.com.** Only move to secondary or tertiary channels when UserInterviews is not a good fit for your study.
</Aside>

<Steps>

1. ### Primary: UserInterviews.com

   Our annual subscription supports up to 150 sessions per year and handles everything in one place: recruitment, screening, scheduling, communication, and compensation.

   - Use screening criteria when setting up the platform
   - Communicate with participants within the platform to verify their fit for the study

2. ### Secondary: State connections

   Use when working with a very specific group already engaged with the State — for example, disability advocacy organizations, LGBTQIA+ organizations, community-based organizations, or state program mailing lists.

   Working with partners can be time-intensive, so plan ahead. Coordinate screening calls through [research@innovation.nj.gov](mailto:research@innovation.nj.gov).

3. ### Tertiary: Independent and manual methods

   Use sparingly and with intention. Options include:
   - Targeted Facebook groups *(always contact moderators first)*
   - The business.nj.gov newsletter
   - LinkedIn ads *(with C+E Lab support)*
   - Multilingual partners or services for non-English speakers

   <Aside type="caution">
   Manual recruitment often attracts participants more motivated by incentives than topic relevance. Screening phone calls are strongly encouraged before any interview.
   </Aside>

</Steps>

## Communicating with participants

All communication with participants should happen through **UserInterviews.com** — including screeners, scheduling, and compensation.

<Aside type="danger" title="Required after every session">
Mark the participant as **"Completed"** in UserInterviews. This step is required for their incentive payment to be disbursed. If an interaction makes you uncomfortable, end the session, contact UserInterviews support, and do not mark it complete until after investigation.
</Aside>

### Informed consent

Participants must complete a consent form before research begins. All consent forms should:

- Explain the purpose and format of the research
- Clarify confidentiality (PII will remain private)
- Request consent to record, with an option to participate without recording
- Remind participants they can withdraw or change consent at any time

## Collecting and managing data

<Aside type="danger" title="PII must always be stored securely">
All personally identifiable information must be saved in the project's **Sensitive Documents folder** in Google Drive, with access restricted to the immediate research team only.
</Aside>

<Steps>

1. ### Store sensitive data

   All PII — names, contact details, recordings, transcripts — must go in the project's Sensitive Documents folder. Never store participant data in general project folders, personal drives, or open shared spaces.

2. ### Record sessions

   - **Remote sessions:** Record through Zoom or Teams, integrated via UserInterviews.
   - **In-person sessions:** Migrate recordings from the recording device to the Sensitive Documents folder promptly.

   Always remind participants not to enter, display, or say PII during the session.

3. ### Handle accidental PII capture

   If PII is accidentally captured in a recording:
   - Sanitize the file by removing the PII
   - Destroy the original recording containing raw PII
   - Retain only the sanitized version

4. ### Prepare recordings for synthesis

   Remove all PII before uploading to Dovetail. Useful tools:
   - **Clipchamp** — Microsoft's web-based video editor for trimming audio and video
   - **Windows Voice Recorder** — built-in tool for recording and trimming audio

5. ### Share findings responsibly

   Share themes, insights, and anonymized quotes only. Never share full recordings, transcripts, or participant names outside the research team.

</Steps>

## Conducting interviews

### Before the session

Begin every session by setting expectations and building trust:

- Introduce yourself and your role
- Explain the purpose of the session in plain language
- Confirm consent (recording or notes-only)
- Reassure participants they can skip any question or stop at any time
- Ask if they need any accommodations

<Aside type="note" title="When observers are present">
Acknowledge that others may be on the call, then reassure the participant: *"You may notice some other names on the call. They're here to quietly observe or take notes — today's session is really just between you and me."*
</Aside>

### Asking good questions

<CardGrid>
  <Card title="One question at a time" icon="approve-check-circle">
    Avoid double-barreled questions. Ask about one thing, get a response, then move on.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Stay neutral" icon="information">
    Don't lead participants toward a particular answer. Use language that leaves the door open.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Keep it open-ended" icon="open-book">
    Encourage richer responses. Avoid yes/no questions wherever possible.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Use their language" icon="translate">
    Mirror the terms participants use instead of imposing jargon or internal terminology.
  </Card>
</CardGrid>

### Observer ground rules

<Aside type="note">
- Stay **off camera** and **on mute** at all times
- Do **not** use chat during the session
- Change your display name to **Notetaker** or **Observer**
- Your role is to listen — let the facilitator lead
</Aside>

### Ending the session

- Ask if participants have any final thoughts
- Thank them sincerely for their time and input
- Remind them how compensation will be handled (via UserInterviews)
- Ask if they'd be open to participating in future research
- Share next steps — how findings may be used or when they might hear from you

## Synthesizing research

All synthesis happens in **Dovetail** — our single source of truth. Do not synthesize in private notes, whiteboards, or personal documents.

<Steps>

1. ### Import your materials

   Upload sanitized transcripts, notes, and media into Dovetail. Only upload files with PII already removed.

2. ### Highlight and tag

   Mark meaningful moments — frustrations, successes, key quotes — with consistent tags such as "pain points," "workarounds," or "positive experience."

3. ### Group and cluster

   Look for themes across participants: similar words, repeated struggles, or patterns in behavior. Notes capture tone and body language; transcripts provide accuracy and detail. Together, they make patterns easier to spot.

4. ### Summarize insights

   Write clear, actionable insights supported by direct participant evidence. Focus on the "so what" — how findings should influence design or decision-making.

5. ### Check for bias

   Make sure your interpretation isn't overly shaped by your own assumptions or a single participant's story.

</Steps>

## Sharing research

<Aside type="tip">
Research is only valuable if it's shared. Balance clarity, respect for participants, and impact in every output.
</Aside>

Every research output should include:

1. **Purpose** — Why we did this research
2. **Methods** — Who we spoke to and how
3. **Key themes and insights** — Supported by anonymized participant quotes
4. **Opportunities or recommendations** — How findings can improve services or inform decisions
5. **Limitations** — Be transparent about sample size, scope, or constraints

### Output formats

<CardGrid>
  <Card title="Insight summaries" icon="document">
    One-page briefs highlighting key findings and recommendations. Can be created and shared directly through Dovetail.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Slide decks" icon="laptop">
    Concise presentations with quotes, themes, and visuals — ideal for stakeholder meetings.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Visual artifacts" icon="star">
    Journey maps, personas, or service blueprints that illustrate complex user experiences at a glance.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Reports" icon="list-format">
    Detailed documentation for complex projects or cross-agency work where depth is needed.
  </Card>
</CardGrid>

<Aside type="note" title="Accessibility matters">
Keep all presentations and reports screen-reader friendly. Use clear, high-contrast visuals, provide alt text for images and diagrams, and write summaries in plain language for diverse audiences.
</Aside>
